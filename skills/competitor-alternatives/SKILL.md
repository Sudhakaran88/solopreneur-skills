## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill when:
- A prospect is actively comparing you to a named competitor
- You want to rank for "[Competitor] alternative" or "[Your Product] vs [Competitor]" searches
- You need a sales page to send to prospects who mention a competitor in discovery
- You're building a bottom-of-funnel SEO content cluster
- You're on a review platform (G2, Capterra) and need positioning to match

Do NOT use this skill for:
- Top-of-funnel awareness content
- Generic "features" pages with no competitive angle
- Situations where you have zero knowledge of the competitor's actual product

## Context Check

Before writing any comparison page, ask:

1. **Who is searching this query?** — A buyer who already knows both products, or someone just becoming aware of the category?
2. **What stage are they in?** — Late-stage (ready to buy) vs. mid-stage (still exploring)
3. **What do you know about the competitor?** — Feature set, pricing, known weaknesses, and who they serve best
4. **Who is your ICP for this page?** — Not "everyone" — the specific person who chose the competitor but might be a better fit for you
5. **What does the competitor do better than you?** — If you can't answer this honestly, you're not ready to write the page

## The 4 Page Formats

### Format 1: You vs. Competitor (`[Your Product] vs [Competitor]`)
**When to use:** You have a well-known competitor with significant search volume. You want to own the narrative when someone Googles your name alongside theirs.

- URL: `/your-product-vs-competitor`
- Target keyword: `[your product] vs [competitor]`
- Tone: Confident, fair, specific
- Differentiator: You control the frame. Use this to highlight your ICP fit, not just features.

### Format 2: Competitor vs. You (`[Competitor] vs [Your Product]`)
**When to use:** The competitor has more brand authority. Searchers are looking from the competitor's lens — "I know Competitor X, who else is like them?"

- URL: `/competitor-vs-your-product`
- Target keyword: `[competitor] vs [your product]`
- Tone: Educational, respectful, direct
- Differentiator: Meet them where they are. Acknowledge why someone might choose the competitor, then clearly explain who you serve better.

### Format 3: Singular Alternative (`[Competitor] Alternative`)
**When to use:** Someone is frustrated with a specific competitor and searching for one replacement. High commercial intent. Often the highest-converting format.

- URL: `/competitor-alternative` or `/best-competitor-alternative`
- Target keyword: `[competitor] alternative`
- Tone: Empathetic to their frustration, solution-focused
- Differentiator: Lead with the pain point that makes people leave the competitor. Use switcher language.

### Format 4: Plural Alternatives (`Best [Competitor] Alternatives` / `[Category] Alternatives`)
**When to use:** You want to capture broader category search volume. Good for programmatic SEO or when you're newer and lack brand search volume.

- URL: `/best-competitor-alternatives` or `/competitor-alternatives`
- Target keyword: `best [competitor] alternatives`, `[competitor] alternatives`
- Tone: Helpful, category-expert
- Differentiator: List 5–8 real alternatives (including honest coverage of competitors). Feature your product as the best fit for your specific ICP — not universally "the best."

## Honest Comparison Principles

This is the most important section. Most comparison pages fail because they're dishonest. Buyers expect bias. When you prove you're not biased, they trust everything else you say.

### What buyers trust:
- Acknowledging competitor strengths: "Competitor X is better if you need enterprise SSO or Salesforce integration"
- Linking directly to the competitor's website (yes, this is a trust signal)
- Listing your own honest cons: "We don't have a mobile app yet"
- Quotes from real users who switched — naming the specific competitor they left
- Pricing transparency: Show real numbers, not "contact us"

### What destroys trust:
- Every feature marked with a green checkmark for you, red X for them
- Vague claims: "We're easier to use" with zero proof
- Feature tables that list obscure features your competitor actually has but you've hidden with fine print
- Screenshots from old versions of the competitor's product
- No mention of who the competitor is better for

### The honest cons rule:
If you cannot name one thing the competitor does better than you, rewrite the page. Buyers will find out. When you tell the truth about one weakness, everything else you claim becomes credible.

## Page Structure

Use this structure for both "vs" pages and "alternative" pages. Adapt section depth based on word count target (800–2,000 words is standard).

### 1. Hero Section
- H1: `[Your Product] vs [Competitor]: Which is right for you?` or `The best [Competitor] alternative for [ICP]`
- Subhead: One sentence positioning for your ICP. Not "we're the best" — "built for [specific person] who needs [specific outcome]"
- CTA: Free trial / demo / start free — above the fold

### 2. ICP Fit Statement
Before any feature comparison, state who each product is best for:
> "[Your Product] is built for [ICP]. [Competitor] is the better choice if [specific use case]."

This signals honesty immediately and pre-qualifies the reader.

### 3. Comparison Table
- Include: Pricing, core features relevant to the ICP, support channels, onboarding, integrations, free trial availability
- Mark with context, not just checkmarks: Instead of ✓/✗, use "Yes (up to 5 seats)" or "Not yet in 2026 roadmap"
- Keep it focused: 8–12 rows max. A 40-row table signals desperation, not thoroughness.
- **The table is necessary but not sufficient.** It answers "does it have X?" — it does not answer "will I regret switching?" Narrative does that.

### 4. Narrative Section: What Makes You Different
- 2–3 short sections explaining your core differentiators in plain language
- Use customer language, not feature names
- Include a screenshot or short GIF for each differentiator
- Pair with a specific stat or proof point

### 5. Switcher Testimonials
This is the single highest-converting element on a comparison page.

Format: `"We switched from [Competitor] because [specific reason]. In [timeframe], we [specific outcome]." — [Name, Role, Company]`

Guidelines:
- The competitor name must appear in the quote — generic testimonials don't work here
- Get 2–3 switcher quotes specifically for this page
- Video testimonials from switchers are 3–5x more credible than text

### 6. Honest Cons + Who Should Stay
> "If you need [X] or [Y], [Competitor] is probably the better fit. Here's our honest take."

This section converts lurkers. People who were on the fence trust you after reading this.

### 7. Pricing Section
- Show real pricing, with a link to your full pricing page
- If you're cheaper: quantify the savings over 12 months for the target company size
- If you're more expensive: explain the ROI or what's included that the competitor charges extra for

### 8. Final CTA
- Primary: Free trial / demo
- Secondary: "See how we compare to [other competitors]" → links to other vs/alternative pages (internal linking for SEO cluster)
- Optional: Live chat prompt ("Switching from [Competitor]? Talk to us — we'll migrate your data free")

## The Feature Table Debate

Feature tables alone don't convert. Here's why:

- Buyers scan the table, assume every vendor shows a biased version, and discount it
- A table answers "what does it have?" — it doesn't answer "will this solve my problem?" or "will I regret switching?"
- Pages that are *only* a feature table perform like informational content (~0.5% CVR) regardless of how high-intent the traffic is

**What to pair with the table:**
- Narrative sections explaining *why* a feature matters for the ICP
- Switcher testimonials that confirm the table's claims with lived experience
- A use case walkthrough: "Here's how [ICP persona] uses [feature] to do [job]"
- An honest "who should choose the competitor" section

The table earns the right to be trusted. The narrative earns the conversion.

## Legal Guardrails

Comparison pages carry legal risk when done carelessly. As a solopreneur, a trademark dispute is costly even if you win.

### What is safe:
- Factual feature comparisons you can verify with a current screenshot or documentation link
- Using a competitor's trademarked name to refer to their product (nominative fair use in most jurisdictions)
- Linking to their pricing page or documentation
- Quoting publicly available information (pricing, feature lists from their site)

### What creates risk:
- Claiming a competitor's product has a bug or security issue you cannot prove
- Using a competitor's logo in a way that implies endorsement or partnership
- Displaying outdated screenshots that misrepresent their current product
- Making claims like "the only [X] that does [Y]" if that's not verifiably true
- Mimicking their brand design in a way that causes confusion

### Practical rules:
1. Screenshot every claim — date-stamp competitor screenshots with a tool like Wayback Machine
2. Add a "last verified" date to your comparison table
3. Never claim a competitor's product cannot do something — say "as of [date], we couldn't find this in their documentation"
4. If a competitor requests removal, evaluate whether the claim is factually defensible before complying

## Common Mistakes

**1. The propaganda page**
Every feature is a green checkmark for you, every limitation is a red X for them. Buyers see through this immediately. It destroys credibility for the entire page.

**2. The G2 dump**
20 pages, each one a different competitor's logo on the same template with the same feature table. Google sees thin content. Buyers see a page that doesn't know them. Both convert at zero.

**3. No ICP specificity**
"We're better than [Competitor]" for everyone = compelling for no one. The page that converts names a specific person with a specific problem who will specifically regret not switching.

**4. Feature table without narrative**
Covered above. Tables create permission to trust. Narrative creates the decision.

**5. No switcher testimonials**
The most common omission. You can fix a mediocre page with two good switcher quotes faster than any other change.

**6. Outdated competitor information**
Competitors update their products. A comparison page that says "Competitor X doesn't have feature Y" when they added it six months ago destroys your credibility.

**7. Ignoring the competitor's strengths**
If you don't mention what the competitor does well, buyers Google it and find out themselves — and now they distrust your entire page.

## Contrarian Takes

**"Linking to your competitor's website helps your page."**
Most marketers refuse to link out to competitors. That refusal signals fear. A confident, direct link to the competitor's pricing page tells readers you're not hiding anything — and they come back to your page with more trust.

**"Admitting you're worse at one thing increases overall conversion."**
An honest "we're not the right choice if you need X" section pre-qualifies readers and converts the right ones at dramatically higher rates. One SaaS found their "Why [Product] might not be right for you" page converted at 13.8%.

**"The best alternatives pages include competitors you'll lose to."**
A "Best [Competitor] alternatives" page that only lists weak alternatives reads as fake. Include the real alternatives — including ones stronger than you in certain areas. You'll rank better (Google rewards comprehensiveness) and convert better (buyers trust the honest framing).

**"Your comparison page doesn't need to rank #1 to generate ROI."**
Even a page ranking #4–7 for a high-intent term can drive meaningful pipeline if it converts well. Optimize for conversion first, then ranking.

## Quick Wins (Do These First)

1. **Identify your 3 most-searched competitor comparisons** — Use Ahrefs or Google Search Console to find "[competitor] alternative" and "[competitor] vs [your product]" queries you already appear for but don't have a dedicated page
2. **Mine your CRM for switchers** — Find the last 10 customers who came from a named competitor. Email them for a 2-sentence testimonial naming the competitor they left.
3. **Audit your competitor's current product** — Sign up for their free trial today. Screenshot the UI. Note what they do well and what they don't. Date-stamp everything.
4. **Write the ICP fit statement first** — Before the full page, write one sentence: "This page is for [specific person] who tried [competitor] and found [specific problem]." Let that sentence guide every word.
5. **Add a "last verified" date to your comparison table** — One small addition that signals you're maintaining the page, not abandoning it.
